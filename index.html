Australia's Climate Chronicles: A Visual Exploration

Motivation:

After waking up to a particularly frosty morning in Melbourne, and as someone from tropical Mauritius, I couldn’t help but wonder how Melbourne’s climate compared to the rest of Australia. So I decided to turn this curiosity into a personal project to learn D3.js, an amazing tool for creating data visualisations.

This choropleth map showcases monthly average temperatures across Australia from January 2000 to June 2024, along with a line plot to compare temperature differences between two cities. It's a fun way for me—and hopefully you—to explore the diverse climates across the country.

The raw temperature data was collected from the Bureau of Meteorology, and the Australian regional boundaries are from Ferocia.

How to use:
  • Hover: Hover over any region to see its postcode and temperature. The legend's colour gradient helps you gauge the temperature range.
  • Slider: Slide through the months from January 2000 to June 2024. The slider even changes colour to match the season!
  • Play/Pause: Press "Play" to watch the temperature trends unfold. Use "Pause" to stop and explore a specific month.
  • Reset: Quickly jump back to January 2000 with a single click.
  • Zoom: Zoom in with "+", zoom out with "-", and reset the map with "⟳".
  • Line Plot: Pick two cities from the dropdowns to compare their temperature trends. The selected cities will light up on the map!
Insights:

The map reveals some interesting trends across Australia! Northern regions like Darwin stay warm year-round, making it the hottest city. Meanwhile, Melbourne is one of the colder spots, only warmer than Hobart. Compared to Melbourne, Canberra stands out with even more extremes—hotter summers and colder winters.

As winter rolls in, the south cools down quickly, especially in the southeast, which you can see in the map's colour shift. When summer arrives, the warmth spreads more slowly to the south, reminding us why Melbourne’s winters feel so long. Maybe it's time to consider a move to the north for a bit more warmth!
